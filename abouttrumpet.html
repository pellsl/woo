A History of the Elusive Trumpetfish

Trumpetfish are named for the famed trumpeter Dizzy Gillespie. Dizzy was the grandson of the King of Tuvalu. He spent his youth in Tuvalu before becoming the musician he is known as today. It was well known that he kept several of these fish as pets. At the time they were known as Nunu fish, but it has become more famously known as the Trumpetfish since he came to prominence on the musical stage.

Trumpetfish Habitat

Trumpetfish are distributed globally in tropical waters. They are not open ocean fish, rather living on coral reefs. They can be found in both near shore waters and in waters up to 30 meters deep, inhabiting bioherms. They are masters of camouflage, often hanging in the water column waiting for prey to come along or swimming amongst grazers to get close to unsuspecting food before pouncing. They will, at times, swim vertically while trying to blend in among vertical coral, such as sea pens and pipe sponges.

How to Identify a Trumpetfish

Trumpetfish are long slender fish, oval in cross-section, growing up to 3 feet long. Their mouth is featured at the end of a long snout. They have one small dorsal fin set back on the body. The tail fin is broad and fanning. They can come in a large range of colors such as yellow, orange, silver, and grey-brown. Additionally, the Trumpetfish is capable of changing its coloring to assist in its ability to camouflage.

Trumpetfish are more often solitary fish, though can be found occasionally in pairs. It would be highly unusual to find them in schools.

Common Mistakes in Identification

Trumpetfish and Cornetfish are members of the family that includes seahorses. The tail on the Cornetfish is not fanned, but is more lunate shaped with a long thin tail filament trailing behind it. The easiest way to remember how to distinguish the two while out swimming is to imagine the instruments they are named for -- a cornet horn is long, slender and constricted, while the trumpet has a more flared bell.

Cornetfish are not the only fish that Trumpetfish can be confused with. They can also be mistaken for juvenile barracudas as they both have long slender bodies. But they are so strikingly different apart from this that it would be hard to make this mistake. For one, barracudas have a large toothed mouth with a significant underbite, and two dorsal fins. Needlefish are more likely to be confused with trumpetfish, but their long, beaklike mouths set them apart. Additionally, Needlefish and Cornet fish are distinctive from the Trumpetfish in that they both travel in large schools of fish.
